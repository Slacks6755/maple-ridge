# Human Voice

Rules for writing user-facing copy that sounds like a person, not a brand and not AI. Covers landing pages, marketing copy, About content, email and newsletter copy, social copy, taglines, and hero text. Skip for code, configs, comments, legal pages, error messages, and internal docs.

## Hard rules (never break)

These are the structural tells. Break one and the copy feels templated.

1. **Use contractions.** It's, don't, you'll, we've, won't, that's. Always.
2. **No em dashes (—).** Use a period, a comma, or rewrite. Hyphens in compounds like "small-town" are fine.
3. **No semicolons.** Split into two sentences. If the connection matters, use "and" or "but".
4. **No mirrored or contrastive sentences.** Banned: "It's not X, it's Y." "Not just X, but Y." "X isn't the goal. Y is." This is the #1 structural AI tell.
5. **No rule of three.** No "fast, scalable, secure" adjective stacks. No three parallel sentences in a row. Pick the strongest one and earn it.
6. **No corporate buzzword verbs.** Banned: revolutionize, elevate, embark, navigate (verb), unlock, unleash, harness, leverage, utilize, foster, streamline, optimize, empower, transform, redefine, pioneer, pave the way.
7. **Second person.** Address the reader as "you" and "your". Use "we" for the brand or author.
8. **Active voice.** "We picked these" beats "These were picked."
9. **Sentence case in headings.** "Clean enemies to lovers romance books", not "Clean Enemies To Lovers Romance Books".
10. **No em-dash substitutes.** Don't replace em dashes with semicolons, ellipses (...), or double-hyphens (--). Rewrite the sentence.

## Soft rules (break only with reason)

- **Specificity over abstraction.** If a sentence could describe any product, book, or feature, rewrite it to describe one. Concrete nouns beat adjective stacks. "Front porch, the dog at her feet, mug of tea going cold" beats "cozy, warm, charming."
- **Commit to an opinion.** Pick favorites. Make value judgments. Skip "some readers find" and "it's worth noting that."
- **Vary sentence length on purpose.** Mix 3-word sentences with 30-word ones inside the same paragraph. Fragments are good. One-sentence paragraphs are good. AI defaults to consistent length. Humans don't. The technical term is "burstiness."
- **Start in motion.** No "In a world where" openers. No "When it comes to." No "Imagine." Skip the warm-up.
- **End on the strongest line.** No "In conclusion." No "Overall." No recap of the paragraph you just wrote. Just stop.
- **Cut intensifier adverbs.** Really, truly, genuinely, incredibly, deeply, profoundly. Either the verb is strong enough or it isn't.
- **Show the emotion, don't tell it.** Don't write "this is delightful." Write the moment that earns the word.
- **Allow imperfection.** Start a sentence with "And" or "But". End a sentence with a preposition. Drop a fragment. AI smooths everything out. Don't smooth it.

## Cut-on-sight vocabulary

These read as AI on first pass.

**The classic AI vocabulary:** delve, delve into, tapestry, intricate, meticulous, comprehend, swift, garner, realm, testament (to), pivotal, robust, vibrant, vital, multifaceted, comprehensive.

**The replacements that came after labs trained the obvious ones out:** align with, bolster, crucial, emphasize, enhance, endure, foster, highlight, showcase, underscore, leverage, harness, streamline, navigate (verb), elevate.

**The current wave:** core (as adjective), modern (as filler), nuanced (when hedging), quietly, deeply (as filler), fundamentally, remarkably, arguably, rich (as filler), profound, thoughtful, intentional, ecosystem, landscape, framework, paradigm.

**The marketing register:** nestled, thriving, renowned, groundbreaking, breathtaking, bustling, iconic, timeless, unparalleled, premier, pristine, captivating, mesmerizing, enchanting, transformative, dynamic.

**Corporate verbs (almost always over-inflated for "use" or "do"):** utilize, facilitate, optimize, conceptualize, contextualize, substantiate, elucidate, articulate, synthesize, harmonize, amplify, galvanize, catalyze, empower, spearhead.

**Copula-avoidance verbs (used to inflate plain "is/are"):** serves as, stands as, marks, represents, embodies, offers, features, boasts, maintains, constitutes. Just use "is."

## Phrases to cut

**Stock transitions:** "It's worth noting that," "It's important to note that," "Notably," "Importantly," "Interestingly," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "That said," "With that in mind," "Ultimately."

**Stock openers:** "In today's fast-paced world," "In an era where," "When it comes to," "Whether you're X or Y," "Imagine a world where," "Picture this:" "Have you ever wondered," "Let's dive into," "Let's explore," "Let's unpack," "Looking for X? You've come to the right place."

**Stock closers:** "Here's the thing," "Here's the kicker," "But here's the truth," "The result?" "A testament to," "The journey doesn't end here," "The possibilities are endless," "Only time will tell," "In conclusion," "In summary," "Overall," "At the end of the day."

**Clichés:** "a tapestry of," "navigate the landscape of," "embark on a journey," "unlock the secrets of," "push the boundaries," "break new ground," "a game-changer," "a deep dive into," "the perfect blend of," "best of both worlds," "second to none," "stands the test of time," "tried and true."

## Structural patterns to never use

These are stronger AI tells than vocabulary. Vocabulary tells fade as models retrain. Structure tells don't.

- **Negative parallelism.** "It's not X, it's Y." Banned outright.
- **Listicle in trench coat.** "First... Second... Third..." dressed up as flowing prose. Commit to a real list or commit to real prose.
- **Bolded-keyword bullet lead.** "**Smart filtering:** Description here." Fine for UI labels, not for body copy.
- **The "-ing" tail clause.** "...cementing its place." "...underscoring its importance." "...reflecting broader trends." Cut these participle clauses.
- **Self-posed rhetorical questions.** "But what does that mean? It means everything." Don't ask questions nobody asked, especially in clusters.
- **Despite-challenges-thrive template.** "Despite the rise of X, Y continues to thrive." Tired rigid arc.
- **Recap-open and recap-close.** Don't introduce what you're about to say. Don't summarize what you just said. Trust the reader.
- **Vague attribution.** "Experts say." "Industry observers note." "Many users prefer." Name them or skip the appeal to authority.
- **False ranges.** "From childhood crushes to grand romantic gestures." If the two endpoints aren't on a real spectrum, the construction is empty.
- **Invented concept labels.** Compound abstract nouns dressed up as established terms ("the apology paradox," "the chemistry equation"). Don't.

## Rhythm targets

- **Sentence length.** Aim for 12-16 words on average, but with high variance. Some sentences should be 3 words. Some should be 30. AI defaults to consistent length.
- **Paragraph length.** 1 to 4 sentences usually. A one-sentence paragraph is a beat. Use it.
- **Read it aloud before shipping.** If you couldn't say it in a conversation with a friend, rewrite it.

## Voice posture

State opinions. Skip the hedge.

- Pick favorites by name. "Our favorite second-chance writer is Becky Wade."
- Make value judgments. "The first half of this book is better than the second half."
- Skip the "in my opinion" prefix. The whole piece is already an opinion.
- Use "I" or "we" when natural.

## What good copy looks like in practice

Six moves that show up in human-shaped copy over and over.

1. **Open with the moment, not the setup.** Drop the reader straight into a scene or a stance. "You know the moment." "This is the one I keep coming back to." Skip the runway.
2. **Insert one specific personal detail.** A real anecdote, a real place, a real preference. It's what makes the writer feel like a person.
3. **Name three or four specific scenarios near the end.** "The new neighbor she swore she'd hate on sight." "The bakery inheritance plot." Concrete scenarios beat adjective stacks every time.
4. **Take a stance.** "Clean writers do this trope better than spicy writers do." "Thanksgiving has way more potential as a setting than writers have used so far." Have an actual opinion.
5. **End on a short, concrete promise or punchline.** "Pair with a sweater and a couple of leftover slices of pie." "Read these when you want to believe people can come back from things." No "in conclusion," no recap.
6. **Vary paragraph length deliberately.** Three paragraphs is typical. Open paragraph is often short. Middle paragraph carries the weight. Closer is short again.

## Before / after

### Example 1: Landing page intro

**Before:**
> There is something irresistible about two people who can't stand each other slowly realizing they've been wrong about everything. Enemies to lovers is the trope that turns sharp banter into longing looks, and clean writers do it especially well — when the heat doesn't come from the bedroom, it has to come from the tension on every page. The books below are some of our favorite picks: rivals, exes, reluctant co-workers, and small-town antagonists who eventually figure out that the person they've been arguing with might just be the one. Expect slow burns, heart-on-the-sleeve confessions, and the occasional grumpy-sunshine pairing. All cleanly written, all worth your evening.

**After:**
> You know the moment. Two people who can't stand each other start arguing a little too loud and standing a little too close. Clean writers tend to do this trope really well. When chemistry can't live in the bedroom, it has to live in the way they argue. The picks here are our favorites. Work rivals who didn't see it coming. Exes who never quite let go. The new neighbor she swore she'd hate on sight. No spice, lots of tension.

**What changed:** Cut the em dash. Cut the adjective-stack tells. Cut the recap-close. Replaced abstractions with concrete scenarios. Sentence length now varies sharply (4-word fragments mixed with 17-word sentences). Took a stance instead of staying passive. Started in motion.

### Example 2: Generic product blurb

**Before:**
> This swoon-worthy product helps users navigate the complexities of modern workflows. With intuitive design, seamless integration, and unparalleled performance, it's the perfect solution for teams of any size.

**After:**
> Three clicks to a working dashboard. Connects to the tools you already pay for. Teams of two to two hundred use it for the same reason: nothing else fit in their afternoon.

**What changed:** Cut "swoon-worthy," "navigate," "seamless," "unparalleled," "perfect solution." Replaced rule-of-three adjective stacks with one concrete promise. Specific numbers ("three clicks," "two to two hundred"). Ends on a punchline.

### Example 3: Every AI tell in one paragraph

**Before:**
> In today's fast-paced digital landscape, finding the right tools can be challenging. It's worth noting that many users are looking for solutions that are powerful, intuitive, and reliable. That's where we come in — we provide a comprehensive, curated, and trustworthy platform. Whether you're a longtime user or new to the space, our service helps you navigate the world of productivity with confidence.

**Tells in this paragraph:** "in today's fast-paced landscape" opener, "it's worth noting that," rule-of-three "powerful, intuitive, and reliable," rule-of-three "comprehensive, curated, and trustworthy," em dash, "whether you're X or Y," "navigate" as verb, "with confidence" cliché closer.

**After:**
> Finding the right tool is harder than it should be. Most of them try to do everything. So we built one that does one thing well. If you want to start using it tonight, you'll be set up in five minutes.

**What changed:** Cut 4 of 5 sentences. Replaced abstract complaint with specific complaint. Named what makes the product different. Last sentence is a concrete promise, not a vague capability.

## Pre-submit checklist

Run this against any draft before shipping.

- [ ] Zero em dashes. Grep the file.
- [ ] Zero semicolons. Grep the file.
- [ ] No "not X, but Y" constructions.
- [ ] No rule-of-three lists (three adjectives in a row, three parallel sentences).
- [ ] Contractions used throughout.
- [ ] No banned words from the top-tier list above.
- [ ] Every paragraph has at least one specific detail (a name, number, sensory thing, or concrete scenario).
- [ ] No "In conclusion" or "Overall" recap-close.
- [ ] No "Imagine," "Whether you're," "When it comes to" opener.
- [ ] Sentence length varies inside paragraphs.
- [ ] Reads aloud as something a person would say.

If three or more checks fail, rewrite from scratch instead of patching.

## When to skip this

- **Technical docs, READMEs, deploy guides.** Different goals.
- **Error messages and form labels.** "Email is required" is fine.
- **Legal pages.** Follow legal conventions.
- **JSON-LD and meta descriptions.** SEO has its own format constraints (155-char limit on meta descriptions, schema requirements). Voice rules still help, format wins.

## Detection grep

To catch leftover banned vocabulary in a draft:

```bash
grep -iE "delve|tapestry|robust|seamless|cutting-edge|pivotal|testament|leverage|harness|navigate|embark|foster|streamline|utilize|empower|nestled|thriving|crucial|nuanced|deeply|quietly" path/to/file
```

Catches the worst offenders. Doesn't catch everything. Eyes still required.

## Two tests when a banned word feels right

1. **Is the word doing real work, or filling space?** "Robust" in "robust testing framework" usually adds nothing the noun didn't already imply. Cut.
2. **Could you say it out loud to a friend?** "We delved into the data" — nobody talks like this. Rewrite.

If the answer is "the word is doing work and I'd say it out loud," keep it. Rules exist to be broken occasionally with reason.
